About me

I'm currently working at Google in the Brain team, researching deep reinforcement learning with applications to combinatorial optimization problems, search and backtracking.

Previously, I was a graduate student in statistics and data science at Stanford University and worked in Stanford's Artificial Intelligence Lab, where I focused on deep reinforcement learning and transfer learning within the Atari domain and from the Atari domain to computer vision tasks.
I have been involved in a variety of machine learning projects such as recommender systems, feature engineering/selection for call records data, multi-label classification with NLP or microfinance.

When I'm not thinking quantitavely you can probably find me mixing music, playing piano, dancing capoeira or simply relaxing. I enjoy social applications of data science, travelling, video games and food.

I'm interested in the algorithmic, computational and theoretical aspects of Artificial Intelligence with an emphasis on Deep and Reinforcement Learning. My main research interest lies in the development of tractable, efficient and practical approaches for Reinforcement Learning agents capable of extracting meaningful representations from rich sensory inputs.
The growing cross-fertilization between Reinforcement Learning and Deep Learning has opened the door to exciting research opportunities. For example, the use of neural networks for representing policies, value functions and models has proven to be a convincing approach to circumvent to some degree the curse of dimensionality from which most RL approaches suffered severely until recently. This has enabled Reinforcement Learning to work in highly dimensional (or even continuous) state and action spaces. However, the marriage between RL and DL goes further. While reinforcement learners use DL as a general toolkit to obtain powerful representations, deep learners can now introduce non-differentiable interactions in their networks and still back-propagate their gradients using techniques from the RL literature. This has allowed to harness the representative power of neural networks to perform new kinds of computations, such as learning how to interface with external memories or use attention mechanisms, and has blurred the distinction between learning models and learning algorithms.
I am confident that advancements in those fields will have an upside for artificial intelligence, pushing the boundaries of what science and technology can accomplish and subsequently improving health, education and quality of life.
